Arthritis

Chronic Mobility Restriction

About

Arthritis is not just one disease, it refers to a collection of diseases which affect joints and other tissues in the body.
It causes pain and also restricts a person's mobility which results in a lower quality of life than others. Arthritis is the Canada's most prevalent chronic disease and also has no proper cure as of yet.
The prevalence of the disease is projected to increase dramatically which is a sign of worry especially due to there being no cure for the disease. Arthritis has over 100 types with the two main types being rheumatoid arthritis and osteoarthritis.
A lot of people with arthritis also have other conditions such as heart disease and diabetes. A large proportion of people with arthritis are also obese and overweight which makes their joint pains even worse due to them being physically inactive ( first website). In general women are at a higher risk or arthritis with 60% of the arthritis cases in Canada being women.

Types Of Arthritis

Osteoarthritis:
This is also called degenerative arthritis and is the most common type of arthritis. With osteoarthritis cartilage in the joints break down which leads to bones rubbing together and joints becoming inflamed. This eventually leads to pain, bone injury and could also result in bone spur formation. Osteoarthritis can affect 1 or 2 joints in the body and can also effect one whole side of the body. Age, obesity and family history are factors which can lead to a person having a higher risk of the disease. Some common symptoms for this kind of arthritis include joint soreness, morning stiffness, lack of coordination and increasing disability.

Rheumatoid arthritis:
This is a type of autoimmune disease in which the body attacks healthy joint tissue. Common symptoms include morning stiffness and joint pain which usually occurs in the same joints on both sides of the body. Other symptoms include difficulty sleeping and numbness, burning and tingling in hands and feet. Several tests can be done to determine if a person has rheumatoid arthritis which includes x rays and other imaging tests.

Cure and treatments

While there is no proper treatment for arthritis yet, scans such as CT scans and MRI'S are conducted to see if a person really has arthritis or not and then some possible treatments currently include medications such as pain killers and other kinds of drugs depending on the type of arthritis a person is diagnosed with.
Physical therapy is also helpful for some kinds of arthritis as exercise improves muscle and joint strength. If none of these measures work then surgeries such as joint repair, joint replacement and joint fusion might be done to treat arthritis. A healthy lifestyle can also help in reducing arthritis symptoms.
Measures such as weight loss and exercise are measures that can be taken to reduce arthritis symptoms or reduce the risk of getting arthritis. Research also suggests that adding turmeric into your diet might also help reduce pain and inflammation caused by arthritis (3rd). Turmeric has a chemical called curcumin which has antioxidant and anti- inflammatory properties which help in reducing pain and inflammation.

Risk factors

Arthritis has both modifiable and non modifiable risk factors. Modifiable risk factors refer to those that can be avoided whereas non modifiable risk factors refer to those risk factors which cannot be avoided.

Modifiable:
Overweight and obesity
Infection
Joint injuries
Occupation
Smoking

Non-Modifiable:
Age
Gender
Genetics and inherited traits

Socioeconomic risk factors

People with a lower socioeconomic status tend to have a higher chance of arthritis as shown in studies. In a study conducted in Taiwan the prevalence of rheumatoid arthritis was looked at and they found that the prevalence of the disease was higher in females and it was also higher in areas of lower income such as suburbs and rural areas. As you moved towards the areas of higher income such as cities the prevalence of the disease decreased which could mean lower income is a possible risk factor for developing arthritis.

Another study which was conducted in Sweden also shows similar trends as it also shows that lower socioeconomic status is associated with a higher risk of developing arthritis. Another study that looks at the relationship between socioeconomic status and arthritis also finds that lower socioeconomic status was initially associated with higher disease activity and pain, however after a year the difference between the higher and lower socioeconomic status group was not evident.

Impact

Canada:
In Canada arthritis is the most prevalent chronic disease and every 1 in 5 people in Canada struggle from this disease. In other words 20% of the Canadian adult population is dealing with arthritis along with approximately 25,000 children and adolescents who are also seeking Healthcare for the disease. By 2040 50% of the population in Canada is projected to have arthritis which is a major issue of concern as this will increase the burden on the Healthcare system such as hospitals and clinics.

Global:
Globally the incidence for rheumatoid arthritis is not really high which is around 3 cases per 10,000 population. This is the average of the whole world which means some places could have a much higher incidence rate whereas some could have an incidence rate lower than this. The rate however is on the rise and is increasing in people who are between the age of 35 and 50, but effects other populations as well. All populations are effected by arthritis, but some are effected more and some are effected less which depends on a few factors such as education, income and socioeconomic status. Things such as gender also matter as women tend to be at a higher risk of developing arthritis than men.

Prevention Strategies

Primary:

These are strategies that aim to prevent the disease before it even starts. For arthritis primary prevention strategies would include:

No smoking
Eating healthy
Controlling weight
Exercise

Secondary:

These are strategies that aim to prevent disease from getting worse and detecting it in early stages:

For arthritis secondary prevention strategies would include seeing a doctor as soon as symptoms are felt so the disease can be detected early and can be treated in the best way possible.

Tertiary:

Tertiary prevention strategies are strategies that aim to minimize the effects of a disease after it occurs. Examples include rehabilitation programs for things such as:

Smoking

Examples for arthritis include:

Rehabilitation programs where people can exercise to increase their joint strength.
Healthy eating, dieting programs so people can reduce their weight.
